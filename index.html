Good Screen vs. Bad Screen
Spiritual Tech

Frank Chimero’s The Good Room is a call-out post I didn’t know I needed. In this presentation of sorts, he poses the question: if technology is a place where we live, shouldn’t we choose to be in lively and nourishing digital environments? I resonate with his rationale in this deck wholeheartedly. With the web becoming more commercialized than ever, what types of digital spaces can we seek refuge in? What’s considered a healthy “digital diet?” What constitutes a “good screen” versus a "bad screen"?

The other week I wrote about divesting from Instagram, which is funny because I thought doing so was such a valiant move on my part, but after reading about Frank’s personal technological regulations, I’m reminded that I still frequent Twitter and Tik Tok. There are a multitude of reasons why I have disdain for Instagram, but I should probably do more self-reflection on why I still hang onto the other two apps, which are equally as evil.

Frank’s bit about the commercialization of the web also made me think about this past week’s lecture where we discussed the four quadrants that digital spaces can be categorized by: 1) agency/networked, agency/siloed, uniform/siloed, and uniform/networked. It also made me realize that there’s a lot of power in publishing your own site in a space that’s cluttered with extractive platforms.

Frank also mentioned that in order for digital environments to be lively and nourishing, they need to be a “space for the soul.” What that looks like requires specific imagination, which is difficult since our imaginations, especially surrounding technology, tend to have a negative flair. This reminded me of what one of my favorite authors, adrienne maree brown described as an “imagination battle.” I’ve never specifically thought about what an ideal virtual environment should and could be. And to be honest, as I’m posing that question to myself right now, I struggle to imagine those specifics.

Divesting from Instagram
Rhizomatic

I read this article called The internet didn’t kill counterculture—you just won’t find it on Instagram, and it really resonated. The piece explores the idea of “counterculture” in an era that’s organized by Big Tech. Last fall, I decided to “divest” from Instagram for several reasons, one of which was because I no longer wanted to be tied to my online identity, an identity that informs and contributes to a toxic algorithm. I can’t help but imagine the loads of intrusive information that these tech behemoths have access to, and it freaks me out. There’s a part in the article that talks about the power dynamics between Big Tech companies and its users. Actual power, according to the article, keeps a low profile. It doesn’t need a social media presence, it owns social media. This idea reminded me of a quote from another reading called The Tyranny of Structureless by Jo Freeman, which discusses how horizontal or flat structures can be problematic in feminist organizing spaces. In the essay, Jo contends:

“The most insidious elites are usually run by people not known to the larger public at all. Intelligent elitists are usually smart enough not to allow themselves to become well known; when they become known, they are watched, and the mask over their power is no longer firmly lodged.”

I think this logic can be applied to Big Tech. These corporations aren’t the overtly evil, top-down, white-collar structures you’d imagine them to be. They come in the form of kombucha-on-tap, flexible-PTO, open-office-plan nonsense. And I think that can arguably be more insidious.

It’s like how people were reasonably overjoyed when Trump was voted out of office, but now we’re back to the same establishment politics that’s been the culprit of a lot of the harm caused prior. “Evil” isn’t as distinguishable anymore, instead, it’s disguised through empty calls for unity and police reform.

Data and Stuff
Server Farm

I just finished reading this New Yorker article about the history of Web archiving. Brewster Kahle, the founder of the Internet Archive and inventor of the Wayback Machine, views the Web as a giant library and doesn't think it should belong to a sole corporation. He believes, "We are building a library that is us."

This quote stuck out to me for a number of reasons. Tangential to the topic of the article, I've been thinking a lot about data privacy, and how through our online activity, we’re contributing to an archive of data collected by data brokers, advertisers, and internet service providers to be exploited by companies, institutions, and the state. But Wayback Machine functions as the opposite — anyone on the internet can contribute to its archive, which kind of (or maybe this is a stretch) restores autonomy back to users. I don't know, maybe it's important to distinguish the power dynamic or imbalance of who is doing the archiving and who is being archived. That is my half-baked, unseasoned thought for today. Maybe I'll elaborate more on this in a future entry.

Earth's Natural Internet
Fungus

I've been thinking about this past week's lecture on the origin of the internet. I was born in 1995 and am the product of unsupervised internet activity, for better or worse. I was on Myspace at a very young age, and like many people, learned how to code through there. Although a lot has changed between when the browser was invented and when Myspace was at its peak, the idea of regular people naively creating websites to reflect who they are for no commercial purpose still felt like the spirit of the internet during that era. That was a good time, even though I didn't understand much of what was going on since I was so young.

I've also been thinking about what the internet looks like, and how it's very much a physical thing as much as it's digital, so much so that the servers, cables, and wires rooted in our earth and in our walls contribute to a significant portion of global carbon emissions. In trying to visualize the internet, I've been looking at different internets beyond the World Wide Web. I recently learned about the Wood Wide Web, in which plants communicate with each other through mycorrhizal fungi. Apparently, fungi is considered the earth's "natural internet." I found that to be pretty neat.
