Final Thoughts?
Refusal

I just read a manifesto called Against software development by Michael Arntzenius, which is apt considering it's the last week of the semester. The third section of the manifesto, which is all about refusing to work for extractive systems (if you have a choice), really resonated with me. Considering that I'm finished with two out of the three semesters of this program, I've been thinking a lot about what type of work I'd like to engage with afterwards. I think I have a clear idea of what I don't want, which is everything the manifesto encouraged to refuse. I've worked for a large corporation before, and I wouldn't want to do that again. But I do want to acknowledge that we live within the strictures of capitalism, and it's not easy to operate outside of them. Maybe that's why the manifesto also stressed to refuse *if you have a choice.* Sometimes having that choice is a privelege. However, I am excited because this past semester introduced me to the possibilities of creative coding and the radical spaces and communities that inhabit this world. I'd like to explore those spaces more.

The other day, I had a brief conversation with John about this, and he posed the question: "do you want to be a developer or an artist?" I think there's a lot to unpack in this question, and I think both identities can exist simultaneously. I still have so much to learn, but this question will definitely inform the way I approach my work.

Sweating Data
Surveillance

Reading Data Sweat by Amanda K. Greene was pretty frightening. Green references Melissa Gregg, an author who coined the term "data sweat," which encapsulates the way in which our seemingly minor and passive online interactions, such as scrolling, mouse movements, and tapping, "leak messily out of our pores as opposed to emerging directly from our machines as a kind of industrial waste product" to be used as data. I had written about data and surveillance in previous entries, but I had no idea that actions as minor as hovering could be exploited as some kind of data point. This point from the essay in particular is unsettling:

"The fact that our own data sweat frequently leaks out unnoticed makes it a particularly powerful resource for generating these subtle “nudges” that strive to operate below conscious awareness."

It's especially unsettling because although there are tools such as VPNs and DuckDuckGo to mitigate surveillance, I wonder if there are tools to prevent my own data sweat from leaking.

Gooeys
White as Neutral

Black Gooey Universe, an essay and exhibition by American Artist, is a necessary critique of how white supremacy and anti-Blackness are embedded in our graphical user interfaces — GUIs or gooeys. Although it's not surprising that our tech is inherently racist (eyeing all of the cis white men in Silicon Valley), I still find this reality disconcerting. American Artist mentions how gooeys historically were a black screen, but in an effort to posit whiteness as neutral, tech behemoths turned screens to white in the spirit of capitalism and corporate productivity. This quote from the essay in particular really stuck out to me, especially considering last week's journal entry on handmade websites:

"Black gooey might then be a platform of slowness (“dragged time”, “colored time”), refusal, thought, complexity, critique, softness, loudness, transparency, uselessness, and brokenness. A planar body that longs for the solitude and vastness of the command-line, yet nuanced and sharp, to usurp and destroy a contemporary hegemonic interface."

This essay also prompts me to dig into and investigate the history behind the tech we use, along with its racial implications. For instance, I recently learned that the p5.js library initially intended to be a Bauhaus drawing library given its primitive shape functions. I'm glad that this isn't entirely the case now given the vibrant community that has formed from this library.

Infrastructure Poetry
Code as Texture

I just learned about a net artist duo called Jodi after reading Alexander R. Galloway's piece on their "infrastructure." Galloway suggests that there are two basic activities that emerge when it comes to digitality: a person may 1) work "on" the digital or 2) work "within" it. In the former mode, which is considered to be modern, infrastructure is everything, which is the throughline of almost all of Jodi's pieces. I especially like this bit where Galloway elaborates on how the material of Jodi's work is simply the material itself:

"...the texture of code and computation takes over, and computing itself—its strange logic, its grammar and structure, and often its shape and color—produces the aesthetic."

It's an interesting framework that I haven't thought of before — code as texture. I guess you could say this concept is similar to the aesthetics of web brutalism, but I've never considered the web's infrastructure, what's under the hood, as something that could have poetic potential.

Divesting from Instagram
Rhizomatic

I read this article called The internet didn’t kill counterculture—you just won’t find it on Instagram, and it really resonated. The piece explores the idea of “counterculture” in an era that’s organized by Big Tech. Last fall, I decided to “divest” from Instagram for several reasons, one of which was because I no longer wanted to be tied to my online identity, an identity that informs and contributes to a toxic algorithm. I can’t help but imagine the loads of intrusive information that these tech behemoths have access to, and it freaks me out. There’s a part in the article that talks about the power dynamics between Big Tech companies and its users. Actual power, according to the article, keeps a low profile. It doesn’t need a social media presence, it owns social media. This idea reminded me of a quote from another reading called The Tyranny of Structureless by Jo Freeman, which discusses how horizontal or flat structures can be problematic in feminist organizing spaces. In the essay, Jo contends:

“The most insidious elites are usually run by people not known to the larger public at all. Intelligent elitists are usually smart enough not to allow themselves to become well known; when they become known, they are watched, and the mask over their power is no longer firmly lodged.”

I think this logic can be applied to Big Tech. These corporations aren’t the overtly evil, top-down, white-collar structures you’d imagine them to be. They come in the form of kombucha-on-tap, flexible-PTO, open-office-plan nonsense. And I think that can arguably be more insidious.

It’s like how people were reasonably overjoyed when Trump was voted out of office, but now we’re back to the same establishment politics that’s been the culprit of a lot of the harm caused prior. “Evil” isn’t as distinguishable anymore, instead, it’s disguised through empty calls for unity and police reform.

Data and Stuff
Server Farm

I just finished reading this New Yorker article about the history of Web archiving. Brewster Kahle, the founder of the Internet Archive and inventor of the Wayback Machine, views the Web as a giant library and doesn't think it should belong to a sole corporation. He believes, "We are building a library that is us."

This quote stuck out to me for a number of reasons. Tangential to the topic of the article, I've been thinking a lot about data privacy, and how through our online activity, we’re contributing to an archive of data collected by data brokers, advertisers, and internet service providers to be exploited by companies, institutions, and the state. But Wayback Machine functions as the opposite — anyone on the internet can contribute to its archive, which kind of (or maybe this is a stretch) restores autonomy back to users. I don't know, maybe it's important to distinguish the power dynamic or imbalance of who is doing the archiving and who is being archived. That is my half-baked, unseasoned thought for today. Maybe I'll elaborate more on this in a future entry.

Earth's Natural Internet
Fungus

I've been thinking about this past week's lecture on the origin of the internet. I was born in 1995 and am the product of unsupervised internet activity, for better or worse. In trying to visualize the internet, I've been looking at different internets beyond the World Wide Web. I recently learned about the Wood Wide Web, in which plants communicate with each other through mycorrhizal fungi. Apparently, fungi is considered the earth's "natural internet." I found that to be pretty neat.
